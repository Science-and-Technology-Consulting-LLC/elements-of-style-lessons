# Who this site is for

A broad audience focused on solving scientific problems.

This curriculum has been taught seven times — at Jackson Laboratory
with the Lifebit team, at ISCB Academy, in NICHD Scholar programmes
on INCLUDE and Kids First, and on the Lifebit Federated Data and
Learning Fabric. The shape it's settled into addresses three things
in every reader.

## Literacy · Capacity · Skill

### 📖 Literacy

The vocabulary, the mental models, the names of the tools. Being
able to *read* a Dockerfile, a Nextflow process, a Typer CLI, a
federated workflow — even before being able to write one. Knowing
what's possible is the first step to choosing to do it.

### 📦 Capacity

The infrastructure — local terminal, conda env, container runtime,
GitHub repo, platform account — that lets you actually run the work.
This is the step that historically had the highest threshold. The
curriculum lowers it deliberately, deck by deck.

### 🛠️ Skill

The doing. Writing the CLI. Writing the module. Stitching the
workflow. Wrapping the CLI as an MCP server. Skill compounds, and
shows up in *how much science you can do per unit of time.*

A fourth thing — **understanding** — emerges when all three are in
place: the ability to *reason about* why a pipeline produced what
it produced, and what to change if a collaborator asks for an
adaptation.

## Specifically, the site is for…

- **People who have to deliver results on a deadline** and don't
  have time to detour into infrastructure for its own sake. Every
  tool here earns its place by saving time later.
- **People who move between platforms** — laptop, ADAPTS, the All of
  Us Workbench, Biowulf, a CAVATICA project, a JupyterHub somewhere.
  The lessons are designed so the muscle memory transfers.
- **People at every skill level**, from "this is my first terminal
  session" to "I've shipped Nextflow pipelines to production." The
  early lessons are gentle on purpose; the later ones still have
  something for the experts.

## Principles the site holds itself to

1. **Educate without disrupting.** Never contradict a director or
   a platform constraint. Add tools the reader can reach for when
   the opportunity arises.
2. **Show, don't just tell.** Every page has runnable code or a real
   artefact. No theory without practice.
3. **Honour the constraints.** When a platform can't `git pull`,
   offer bridge patterns. When a workflow has to copy/paste, make
   copy/paste reproducible.
4. **Be warm.** Working scientists already have enough sources of
   stress. The site is allowed to sound like a person.

## What this site is *not*

- A complete reference for any one tool. Pointers to the official
  docs are everywhere.
- A prescription. The right way to work is the way that ships
  science. The site offers suggestions, not commandments.
